IGAD: Information-Geometric Anomaly Detection via Scalar Curvature of Statistical Manifolds
Omry Damari · Independent Researcher · 2026

Abstract
IGAD: an anomaly detection method that scores observations by measuring how much the local scalar curvature of the Fisher-Rao manifold deviates from a reference distribution. The scalar curvature of exponential family manifolds is governed by the squared norm of the third cumulant tensor — the multivariate generalization of skewness. This means IGAD is inherently sensitive to distributional shape anomalies that leave mean and variance nearly unchanged but alter higher-order structure. We prove that for any exponential family, the IGAD score decomposes into a skewness-contrast term and a metric-volume correction, and we characterize the precise conditions under which IGAD provably dominates Mahalanobis-type detectors. We evaluate IGAD on both synthetic shape-anomaly tasks and standard benchmarks, demonstrating that it fills a gap left by location-scale methods whenever the anomaly signature is concentrated in the third cumulant.

1. Introduction
1.1 The Problem
The vast majority of statistical anomaly detectors operate on one of two principles: distance from a center (Mahalanobis distance, Isolation Forest, LOF) or likelihood under a fitted model (density estimation, one-class SVM). Both are fundamentally sensitive to where a point lies relative to the bulk of the data — its location and, to some extent, its scale context.
But there exists a class of anomalies that these methods systematically miss: observations drawn from distributions that share the same mean and similar variance as the reference population but differ in shape — specifically, in skewness and higher-order cumulant structure. Consider a manufacturing process where sensor readings follow a Gamma(9, 3) distribution under normal operation. A degradation mode that shifts the process to Gamma(1.5, 0.5) produces readings with the same mean (3.0) but dramatically different skewness (1.63 vs 0.67). Individual readings from the degraded process fall well within the support of the normal distribution. Distance-based detectors see nothing unusual. But the local statistical geometry has changed.
1.2 The Gap
Information geometry provides a natural Riemannian structure on families of probability distributions via the Fisher-Rao metric. The curvature of this manifold encodes how the geometry of the family varies across parameter space. Specifically, the scalar curvature is controlled by the third cumulant tensor — the object that generalizes skewness to the multivariate parametric setting.
This curvature has been studied extensively in two contexts:
In information geometry: Amari (1985), Amari & Nagaoka (2000), and others derived curvature formulas for statistical manifolds, primarily to understand the geometry of estimation and the role of dual connections. The Christoffel-symbol identity Γ_{ij,k} = ½T_{ijk} and the cancellation of fourth cumulants in the Riemann tensor are well-established results for Hessian metrics.
In thermodynamic geometry: Ruppeiner (1979, 1995) used the scalar curvature of the entropy Hessian as a diagnostic for phase transitions — flat geometry corresponds to non-interacting systems, and curvature singularities signal critical behavior. This is the closest conceptual precursor to our work.
What is missing: No one has proposed using the scalar curvature difference between a reference point and a local estimate as a general-purpose anomaly detection score on Fisher-Rao manifolds. The idea of curvature-as-anomaly-signal exists in graph analysis (CurvGAD, 2025, using discrete Ollivier-Ricci curvature) and in embedding-based methods (Curved Geometric Networks, 2024), but these operate on fundamentally different geometric objects — graph curvature and embedding-space curvature, respectively — not on the intrinsic Riemannian curvature of parametric statistical models.
This paper fills that gap. We define IGAD as R(θ_ref) − R(θ̂(z)), prove its decomposition in terms of skewness contrast, characterize its power against shape anomalies, and demonstrate both its strengths and its limitations on real data.
1.3 Contributions

IGAD score definition: A principled anomaly score based on scalar curvature deviation on Fisher-Rao manifolds. (Section 3)
Skewness decomposition theorem: We prove that IGAD decomposes into a third-cumulant contrast term and a metric-volume correction, making precise why IGAD detects shape anomalies. (Section 4)
Dominance conditions: We characterize the regime where IGAD provably outperforms Mahalanobis distance: when the KL divergence between normal and anomalous distributions is small but the third-cumulant norm difference is large. (Section 5)
Honest failure analysis: We identify three failure modes — 1D families (R ≡ 0), constant-curvature manifolds, and anomalies that are purely location-scale shifts. (Section 6)
Empirical evaluation: Synthetic shape-anomaly benchmarks and selected ODDS datasets. (Section 7)

3. The IGAD Score
3.1 Definition
Given:

A reference dataset X_ref from which we estimate θ_ref via MLE
A test point z with local neighborhood N(z) in the reference data
A local parameter estimate θ̂(z) = MLE on N(z)

The IGAD anomaly score is:
IGAD(z)=R(θref)−R ⁣(θ^(z))\text{IGAD}(z) = R(\theta_{\text{ref}}) - R\!\left(\hat{\theta}(z)\right)IGAD(z)=R(θref​)−R(θ^(z))
Higher IGAD score → more anomalous.
3.2 Why This Definition
The score measures how much the local statistical geometry near z differs from the global geometry of the reference distribution. Unlike distance-based scores, which measure where z falls in sample space, IGAD measures how the shape of the distribution itself has changed in the neighborhood of z.
Crucially, R(θ) is an intrinsic geometric invariant — it does not depend on the parametrization of the family, only on the probability distributions themselves. This means IGAD is invariant under reparametrization of the exponential family, a property that distance-based methods in parameter space do not enjoy.
3.3 Computational Procedure
Algorithm: IGAD
Input:  X_train (reference data), X_test (test data), 
        family F, k (neighborhood size)
Output: scores ∈ ℝ^|X_test|

1. θ_ref ← MLE(X_train) under F
2. R_ref ← ScalarCurvature(F.log_partition, θ_ref)
3. Build k-d tree on X_train
4. For each z in X_test:
   a. N(z) ← k nearest neighbors of z in X_train
   b. θ̂(z) ← MLE(N(z)) under F
   c. R_z ← ScalarCurvature(F.log_partition, θ̂(z))
   d. score(z) ← R_ref − R_z
5. Return scores
Complexity: O(n_test · (k·log(n_train) + C(d))), where C(d) is the cost of computing scalar curvature in d dimensions (dominated by the O(d³) tensor contractions).

IGAD fires when the local skewness content differs from the reference — exactly the signal that location-scale methods miss.

Informal statement: When anomalies have similar first and second moments but different third-cumulant structure, IGAD detects what Mahalanobis cannot.
Example: Gamma(α₀, β₀) vs Gamma(α₁, β₁) with α₀/β₀ = α₁/β₁ (same mean). The Mahalanobis distance scales as |1/α₀ − 1/α₁|. The curvature difference depends on the full third-cumulant structure, which scales differently. For fixed mean and α₁ ≪ α₀, IGAD diverges while Mahalanobis grows slowly.

6. Failure Modes — When IGAD Does Not Work
Honest characterization of limitations is essential.
6.1 One-Dimensional Families (R ≡ 0)
Any 1D Riemannian manifold has zero scalar curvature. For univariate exponential families parametrized by a single natural parameter (Poisson, Exponential, Bernoulli), the Fisher-Rao manifold is 1-dimensional and flat. IGAD produces identically zero scores.
Mitigation: Embed the 1D family in a higher-dimensional family (e.g., extend Poisson to a generalized Poisson with dispersion parameter).
6.2 Constant-Curvature Manifolds
Some families have scalar curvature that is constant across the entire parameter space (e.g., certain symmetric families). In such cases, R(θ_ref) = R(θ̂(z)) for all z, and IGAD scores are identically zero.
Detection: Check numerically whether R varies across the parameter space before deploying IGAD.
6.3 Pure Location-Scale Anomalies
If anomalies differ from normal data only in mean or variance (not in shape), IGAD has no advantage over simpler methods. In fact, it may be worse, since it ignores the very signal (location shift) that Mahalanobis captures directly.
Recommendation: IGAD is a complement to location-scale detectors, not a replacement. In practice, combine IGAD with a Mahalanobis-type score.
6.4 Estimation Noise in Small Neighborhoods
The local MLE θ̂(z) from k neighbors is noisy. For small k, this noise can dominate the curvature signal. For large k, the estimate is smooth but loses locality.
Practical guidance: k should be chosen by cross-validation. In our experiments, k ∈ [20, 50] works well for n_train ≥ 500.

8. Related Work
8.1 Information Geometry for Statistics
Amari (1985) established the differential-geometric framework for statistical manifolds. Amari & Nagaoka (2000) is the standard reference. The scalar curvature formulas we use are consequences of the Hessian-metric structure of exponential families.
8.2 Ruppeiner Geometry (Conceptual Precursor)
Ruppeiner (1979, 1995) used scalar curvature of the entropy Hessian to diagnose thermodynamic phase transitions. The conceptual parallel is direct: curvature singularity → something unusual is happening. IGAD generalizes this idea from equilibrium thermodynamics to statistical anomaly detection, and from singularity detection to continuous scoring.
8.3 Curvature-Based Anomaly Detection on Graphs
CurvGAD (Grover et al., 2025) detects graph anomalies via Ollivier-Ricci curvature — a discrete notion of curvature on edges, unrelated to Fisher-Rao geometry. Curved Geometric Networks (Hong et al., 2024) embed data in constant-curvature spaces and compute anomaly scores from the embeddings. Neither operates on the intrinsic curvature of parametric statistical families.
8.4 Classical Anomaly Detection
Mahalanobis distance, Isolation Forest (Liu et al., 2008), LOF (Breunig et al., 2000), and one-class SVM (Schölkopf et al., 2001) are the standard baselines. All are primarily sensitive to location and scale, not to distributional shape.

9. Discussion and Future Work
What we have shown: Scalar curvature of Fisher-Rao manifolds provides a principled, parametrization-invariant anomaly score that is sensitive to skewness — the distributional feature most invisible to standard methods.
What remains open:

Nonparametric extension: IGAD requires choosing an exponential family. Can we define a model-free curvature score using kernel density estimators or normalizing flows?
Optimal neighborhood size: Rigorous analysis of the bias-variance tradeoff in local MLE estimation for curvature computation.
High-dimensional scaling: The third cumulant tensor has O(d³) entries. For large d, sparse or low-rank approximations to T are needed.
Combination with distance scores: A formal framework for combining IGAD with Mahalanobis distance to get a detector that is sensitive to both location-scale and shape anomalies.


10. Conclusion
The gap between information geometry and anomaly detection has existed for decades. The mathematical tools — Fisher metric, third cumulant tensor, scalar curvature — have been available since the 1980s. The observation that scalar curvature signals "something interesting happening" has been used in thermodynamic geometry since the 1970s. What was missing was the explicit construction of an anomaly score from curvature deviation on general exponential families, and the theorem that this score is fundamentally a skewness contrast.
IGAD fills this gap. It is not a universal anomaly detector — it has clear, documented failure modes. But for the class of anomalies where the signal lives in distributional shape rather than location or scale, it provides something that no existing method does: a principled, geometrically motivated score with a precise mathematical interpretation.
